Lesson 1 / 4

What is algorithmic trading?

Algorithmic trading ("algo trading") is software that buys and sells based on a fixed set of rules, without a human pressing the button each time.

A rule looks like: "If asset X drops 0.3% in 60 seconds, buy a small amount; sell when it bounces 0.1%." The computer applies that rule consistently — no emotion, no fatigue, no second-guessing.

Three things to know:

  • It removes emotion from execution — not from the strategy.
  • It needs data — systems read exchange price feeds via APIs in milliseconds.
  • It represents most of the market volume in major financial hubs.
Lesson 2 / 4

How arbitrage works (the mechanic)

Example: At 14:02:03 Asset quotes €60,000 on Exchange A and €60,012 on Exchange B. An arb bot buys on A, sells on B, captures the €12 gap (minus fees), and the prices converge.

Why the gap exists:

  • Different liquidity pools on each venue.
  • Different order flow at the exact moment of the snapshot.
  • Network latency between global exchanges.
Lesson 3 / 4

Risk, drawdown, and "daily return"

If somebody advertises "X% per day, guaranteed," treat it as a red flag — not because it's impossible, but because it's almost never the full picture.

Three concepts every reader should know:

  • Volatility: How much a price moves up and down. High volatility means more potential gain — and more potential loss.
  • Drawdown: The drop from a peak to a low. A strategy with high historical gains may also have temporary deep drawdown periods.
  • Risk of ruin: The probability that a streak of losses impacts the account. It depends on position sizing, not just edge.
Lesson 4 / 4

What AI can (and can't) do in markets

AI in finance is real — but narrower than headlines suggest.

What it does well:

  • Pattern detection at scales no human can match — reading millions of order-book snapshots per second.
  • Speed — reacting to a price move in microseconds.

What it doesn't do:

  • Predict the future: Statistical models say "based on past data, X is more likely than Y," not "X will happen."
  • Eliminate risk: Better execution does not mean zero negative trades.
